directed angles

∠ACB=∠ADB∠ACB=∠ADB but ∠ABC=180−∠ADC∠ABC=180−∠ADC. But sometimes theorems work for any four points on a circle. Wouldn’t it be nice to be able to use these theorems without having to check where on a circle the points lie?

A concrete example is as follows: the reflection of HH, the orthocenter, of any triangle ABCABC lands on the circumcircle.

How would you prove this? Note that since ∠AFH+∠AEH=180∘∠AFH+∠AEH=180∘, we know that AEFHAEFH are cyclic. Thus ∠BH′C=∠BHC∠BH′C=∠BHC (reflections) =∠FHE=180∘−∠FAE=∠FHE=180∘−∠FAE so by our good old cyclicity rule, we know ABH′CABH′C are cyclic.

But wait!

What’s going on here?

The above argument obviously breaks down. A few things are mentioned that just aren’t quite true. What changed?

Well, obviously the relative positions of the points changed. H′H′ doesn’t lie between BB and CC anymore, so you can’t use the ∠BH′C+∠BAC=180∘∠BH′C+∠BAC=180∘ argument — in fact that’s not even true.

Stuff like this happening is known as “diagram dependence”, and is basically when you assume things in your diagram are true. For instance, if you assume two lines meet at a point, when really they could be parallel, or when you assume that two points lie on the same side of a line, when really they could lie on opposite sides.

This is where, you guessed it, we introduce the concept of

directed angles

Let’s do this.

Directed angles work as follows:

  • ∠ABC=−∠CBA∠ABC=−∠CBA (compare this to directed lengths, or if you prefer, vectors)
  • angles are taken mod 180
  • ∠ABC∠ABC is how far you have to rotate line (not ray) ABAB around BB to coincide with line BCBC

Pretty simple right?

Another pedant’s note. Ignore if not a pedant. Okay, some of you may have noticed i said rotate. Which direction? It really doesn’t matter; clockwise or anticlockwise, but stick with it. Don’t change direction in the middle of a proof otherwise you’ll have to deal with all kinds of hell. I think the mathematical standard is always rotate anticlockwise, so if you wanna conform then basically a directed angle is how much you have to rotate line ABAB anticlockwise to reach line BCBC. Thanks.

Anyways, for the rest of the page, all angles are directed. If you were writing up a solution for a competition or something, and you wanted to use directed angles, just a one-line note at the top saying “all angles are directed” is enough.

Let’s establish some lemmas. All proofs are left as exercises.

Lemma line

ABCABC are on a line (in any order) if and only if ∠DBA=∠DBC∠DBA=∠DBC.

Lemma circle

ABCDABCD are on a cline (circle or line) iff ∠ACB=∠ADB∠ACB=∠ADB

Lemma point

For any four points A,B,C,PA,B,C,P, ∠APB+∠BPC=∠APC∠APB+∠BPC=∠APC

Lemma triangle

In triangle ABCABC, ∠ABC+∠BCA+∠CAB=0∠ABC+∠BCA+∠CAB=0.

Pretty basic right?

Time to give this a whirl:

For the first question, how would you do it? Notice that ∠AEH=∠AFH∠AEH=∠AFH by definition, so AEFHAEFH are cyclic. But HFCHFC are collinear so ∠BH′C=∠CHB∠BH′C=∠CHB (can you see why this is true?) =∠FHB=∠FHE=∠FHB=∠FHE (as HBEHBE collinear) =∠FHE=∠FAE=∠BAC=∠FHE=∠FAE=∠BAC, where the last few equalities come from cyclic quadrilaterals and collinear points. Notice that, by using directed angles, I don’t actually care what the diagram looks like — they’re always gonna be equal, regardless, and since lemma circle is an iff relationship, we know that the original theorem is true.

Time for some practise!

I mean, directed angles shouldn’t be something else you have to “practise”. You should be using directed angles on “regular” questions too. Get into good habits early!

A word of advice — never halve directed angles. Doubling them is fine, but never ever halve them. If you have to halve one, then… it gets messy, but if you’re doing questions hard enough for you to have to halve directed angles, then you should be fine.

And another piece of advice: Try drawing these diagrams with geogebra (tip: if you’re doing geo, click settings at the top right and turn off the axes and grid. Those aren’t needed) and moving the given points around. There are so many weird configurations for these questions that there’s almost no way you’d be able to cover all of them using “standard” geo.
