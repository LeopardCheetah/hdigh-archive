geombinatorics

Geometrical combinatorics is one of the more colourful types of mathematics. Much like ordinary combinatorics, it has relatively few standard techniques involved (probably even more so than normal combinatorics). Most of the time it comes down to drawing a single diagram that kills the problem. I don’t really know how to finish introductions so i’m just gonna jump into examples.

Every point in the plane is coloured one of two colours. Prove that there are two points with distance 1 apart which have the same colour.

To solve this one, draw an equilateral triangle with side length 1. There are two colours but three vertices, so two of the vertices have the same colour, and we’re done.

Every point in the plane is coloured one of two colours. Prove there is an equilateral triangle whose vertices are all the same colour.

Assume for contradiction that this is not the case. As from the previous question, we can find two points with distance 1 that are the same colour. Let’s call this colour red, and the two points in question D and E.

Now D and E are both red. Since no equilateral triangles of one colour exist, we know B and G are blue. Now BFG is equilateral, but B and G are both blue, so F is red. So we now know that both E and F are red, which tells us C and H are blue. Now from triangle ABC, because both B and C are blue, A must be red. But now ADF is an equilateral triangle, and A, D, F are all red. This is a contradiction, so an equilateral triangle must exist.

Bonus question:

Given a triangle T, players A and B play a game. In a move, A picks a point, and B colours it either red or blue. If three points of the same colour form a triangle similar to T, A wins. Prove that A can guarantee a win in a finite number of moves.

hint:

use the previous question, but with triangle T instead of equilaterals

Most colouring combinatorial geometry questions come down to finding a similar configuration or trick. There are a few other types of combi-geo problems, like the following:

5 points are given in a square of side length 2. Prove that two of these points are at most 2–√2 apart.

This is a classic application of the pigeonhole principle. Divide the square into four squares of length 1. One of these squares must contain two points, and these points have a distance at most 2–√2, as required.

Basically, combinatorics is a really diverse area and combinatorial geometry is no different. Just try problems if you wanna get the hang of it — basically, just try colouring stuff, or drawing shapes. A simple diagram ought to solve most questions.

Alright, that’s enough blathering. Time for problems.

questions
  1. Four points are given in a square with side length 114114. Is it always possible to find a square with side length 11 inside this square that contains none of these points?
  2. Every point in the plane is assigned a real number. Given that the sum of the values of the vertices of any square is 0, must every number be 0?
  3. Every point in an infinite square grid is coloured one of 2019 colours. Prove there is a rectangle with sides parallel to the grid such that the corners have the same colour.
  4. Every point in the plane is coloured one of two colours. Prove that there is either a monochromatic equilateral triangle of side length 1, or side length 3–√3.
  5. We say that a finite set S of points in the plane is balanced if, for any two different points A and B in S, there is a point C in S such that AC=BC. We say that S is centre-free if for any three different points A, B, C in S, there is no point P in S such that PA=PB=PC.
    (a) Show that for all integers n≥3n≥3, there exists a balanced set consisting of nn points.
    (b) Determine all integers n≥3n≥3 for which there exists a balanced centre-free set consisting of nn points.
  6. A configuration of 4027 points in the plane is called Colombian if it consists of 2013 red points and 2014 blue points, and no three of the points of the configuration are collinear. By drawing some lines, the plane is divided into several regions. An arrangement of lines is good for a Colombian configuration if the following two conditions are satisfied:
    i) No line passes through any point of the configuration.
    ii) No region contains points of both colors.
    Find the least value of k such that for any Colombian configuration of 4027 points, there is a good arrangement of k lines.
  7. A rectangle DD is partitioned in several (≥2≥2) rectangles with sides parallel to those of DD. Given that any line parallel to one of the sides of DD, and having common points with the interior of DD, also has common interior points with the interior of at least one rectangle of the partition; prove that there is at least one rectangle of the partition having no common points with DD’s boundary.
  8. In the example game far above, find the minimum number of moves required for A to win.
  9. Let PP be a convex polygon. Find all functions f:R2→Rf:R2→R such that for any polygon A1A2…AnA1A2…An similar to PP, we have ∑i=1nf(Ai)=0∑i=1nf(Ai)=0.
  10. Let n be a positive integer coprime to 6. We colour every vertex of a regular n-gon with one of three colours, such that there are an odd number of vertices of each colour. Show there is an isosceles triangle whose vertices are all different colours.
