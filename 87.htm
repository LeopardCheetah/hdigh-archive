inversion

Formally, inversion is a bijection from the plane with a point at infinity to – not today, strikethrough guy.

Inversion is basically reflection in a circle. If you invert around a circle with centre OO and radius rr, then the point PP gets sent to the point P′P′ on the ray OPOP such that OP×OP′=r2OP×OP′=r2. Most of the time the radius isn’t important, because changing the radius will just expand or shrink the whole diagram from OO.

This is good and all, but how is this helpful in any way? Inversion is powerful because it can transform a lot of circles into lines, which are generally easier to work with. Let’s see how this works.

Lemma Similarity

If AA and BB invert to A′A′ and B′B′ after an inversion from OO, then △OAB∼△OB′A′△OAB∼△OB′A′ (note the reversal of orientation).

Proof: Note that ∠AOB=∠B′OA′∠AOB=∠B′OA′, because B′B′ is on OBOB and A′A′ is on OAOA. Also,

OB′OA′=r2OB−1r2OA−1=OAOB.OB′OA′=r2OB−1r2OA−1=OAOB.
So we’re done by SAS.

Importantly, this means ∠OAB∠OAB = ∠OB′A′∠OB′A′.

Lemma Cline

Call an object a cline if it is a circle or a line. Then clines invert to clines.

To prove this, fix points AA and BB on your cline. Now for any point PP on your cline, the directed angle ∡APB∡APB is constant. Then use the similarity thing from before to consider what happens to A,P,BA,P,B under inversion, and show that ∡B′P′A′∡B′P′A′ is constant.

A key thing to note is that any line through OO inverts to itself, and any line not through OO inverts to a circle through OO. Also, circles through OO invert to lines.

Hopefully you get how inversion works now. There’s one last type of inversion that’s useful:

Root-bc inversion

In triangle ABCABC, take an inversion at centre AA and radius AB⋅AC−−−−−−−√AB⋅AC. Then reflect everything over the angle bisector of ∠BAC∠BAC.

It should be easy to see that BB and CC swap. In particular, line BCBC and the circumcircle swap.

Prove that OO and HH are isogonal conjugates (i.e. the bisector of ∠OAH∠OAH passes through II (and similar results for BB and CC).

Consider where OO goes to, under a root-bc inversion. Since △AOB△AOB is isosceles, by lemma similarity, we must have △ACO′△ACO′ isosceles, i.e. AC=CO′AC=CO′. Similarly, AB=BO′AB=BO′, so O′O′ is the reflection of AA over BCBC. In particular, AO′AO′ is perpendicular to BCBC and so passes through HH. So line AOAO is sent to line AHAH; i.e. the angle bisector of ∠OAH∠OAH passes through II, as required.
