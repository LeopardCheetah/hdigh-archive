hacked

In case you missed it, scientists made a breakthrough a few days ago on the nature of the universe. I mean, us programmers knew for years that the universe is just a program, but scientists have finally “proven” it.

In essence, they did some particle colliding and proved that something fishy was going on, which they (yes, how clever of them) identified as memory errors. You know, when you wanna delete something on your computer, and your computer is like “sorry, that file is in use”? They tried to ensure that never happened, but it turns out even the gods aren’t perfect programmers.

As far as we can tell, the universe is running on some exotic alien hardware with virtually unlimited storage and precision. Nevertheless, there are other limitations which haven’t been patched yet, which are really actually quite difficult to abuse. But while the scientists were busy making sure we were right, we were busy hacking the universe.

I present to you:

The Universe Hacking Guide, v1.0

a foreword on ticks

The universe runs on ticks, which happen extraordinarily quickly. The state of the universe is saved every tick, and if an error occurs (such as two particles exactly overlapping), the universe reverts back to an earlier save and “does some quantum magic”. This was the mechanism via which the nature of the universe was determined. Particles can go at the speed of light locally. Spacetime can bend. Ergo, particles can “appear” to go faster than the speed of light.

You know, the weird thing is, the people who programmed this place somehow didn’t think of that. Or they did and decided that it was rare enough not to matter. Or they were just lazy.

A note on these glitches – not all of these are actually direct glitches; most of them are just applications of previously discovered glitches which were noteworthy enough to get their own section.

Anyways, onto our first glitch:

glitch 1: saveglitch

hacking

If a particle, from a global point of view, appears to have gone faster than the speed of light, it gets sent back in time. Normally spacetime distortions are so small that these effects are unnoticeable. However, in particle colliders where these distortions are purposely induced (even then, spacetime only expands by about a trillionth the width of a proton) and amplified via mirrors, more particles are generated inside the universe.

What’s actually happening isn’t that the particles are being sent back in time per se (because the past doesn’t actually “exist”). What the particle does is go to the file address, or whatever, of “the past” and edit that. This file address happens to be where the save files are stored, which results in a duplicate particle being created. Normally the old particle is overwritten because the two particles are too close together to have occurred naturally (an in-built safety mechanism). I mean, technically it’s still possible for the particles to naturally occur that close together, but really, even in a supernova you’d only get like one or two of those events, if that, just because of how unimaginably tiny these lengths are.

However, sometimes it’s possible to move the particles slightly more, just enough that the engine decides that the particles are far enough apart. The minimum distance is called the true planck length (yeah we decided to honor planck and confuse those scientists more), and we’re not even sure as to its order of magnitude yet.

Anyways, all in all, if this is carried out successfully, the savefile from the past gets altered, and when reloaded, has been corrupted.

reloading

Reloading the savefile is really easy. It’s something to do with using quantum mechanics to violate conservation laws. For instance, it’s widely known that if you manage quantum entangle two particles which you know nothing about, and measure their spins, you’ll find them to correlate, even if you separate the particles beforehand so that light doesn’t have time to transmit the information about one particle to the other.

What’s really happening is a pseudo-local-variable-theory, where the particles each “randomly” choose a state. Later on, if the program detects an error state, where conservation doesn’t apply, it backtracks — and here’s the important thing, it only backtracks locally — to the savefile from before this choice took place.

This means that you can make a savefile restoration device — a SRD — extraordinarily easily. All you do is entangle ten particles, move them apart, and when the time comes, you measure them. Ten particles gives a 99.9% chance of success. Twenty particles gives a 99.9999% chance of success. It’s just probability.

We have some which are small handheld, and disrupt a space of about a cubic decimeter. The rule of thumb is that the time it takes for light to travel from one end of the device to the other is roughly the radius of the sphere of influence.

Put another way, the size of your device is approximately the same as the size of the area it affects.

The devices we’re currently using are single-use before they have to be reset, but we’re hoping to have multi-use devices by the end of the year.

Anyways, these by themselves do virtually nothing, because the savefile from the past and the present are often pretty much exactly the same, so nothing really changes. But when combined with something else, you can start glitching other things.

glitch 2: energy chamber

We put two atomically-aligned mirrors inside a vacuum, and ran a beam of light between them, back and forth. Next, we induce gravitational waves through the chamber. Yeah, we have a big budget.

Anyways, what happened was that we bugged the savefile from the past with trillions of new photons. At the same time, we ran a very large savefile restoration device (SRD, remember?), loaded the old save, and the beams of light were amplified by a miniscule amount. This resulted in a tiny amount of extra energy, around an estimated 0.001%. Pretty pathetic if you ask me, enough to be dismissed as experimental error.

However, we set it up properly and attempted it a few more times. The next time, we got six thousand SRDs and did it again. The resulting light ray was 4.8% more powerful than before, although the gravitational wave was a lot more powerful and did scatter some of the…

…oh, alright, you don’t care about the experiment, only the glitches. I guess I’ll skip to the end.

With a dielectric mirror operating via total internal reflection (by choosing the materials so as to maximise the incidence angles where reflection occurs), it’s possible to create small spheres, with light bouncing around in each. Put them in the charge chamber — a chamber we made, by the way — which is constantly bathed in gravitational waves and SRDs — and after leaving it there for a bit, it’ll be full of energy! If you leave it there too long, it’ll explode eventually.

Don’t worry; if it’s any reassurance, the spheres themselves are quite weak so they don’t have much pent-up energy when they explode. Only enough to take out half the laboratory, as we found out the hard way when somebody forgot to take the spheres out.

That was a tricky one to explain.

Anyways, these energy capsules can be used to power much more high-tech machinery. Incidentally those scientists who “proved” that the universe was a simulation ran their particle collider on energy spheres. I mean, seriously, they were the ones who got credit for proving it, even though they knew energy spheres were a thing? Oh, sure, when you don’t understand something just call it zero-point energy and steal all the credit.

Sorry for ranting again.

Anyways, now we have energy spheres. What next?

glitch 3: rolling the dice

For a long time, we messed around with trying to get these energy spheres to do bigger and bigger things, but we eventually hit a ceiling. Our containment technology has a limit on the maximum energy density it can store. Even with our best optimisations, it’s impossible to get a mirror with 100% reflectivity, and attempts to store energy in some other forms is always bounded by the laws of physics. As things stood, we didn’t have enough ways to break the universe.

That is, until we worked out how to mess with probabilities.

On its own, an SRD is only capable of creating a tiny influence, affecting only a few particles. But if we accept quantum physics, fundamental fields might actually be probabilistic. Turns out that the aliens have a randomisation system that’s synced with their perception of time, not ours. So by placing some detector condition on a system of SRDs, we can keep reloading the universe until a condition is fulfilled (by the way, this proves that the universe is in fact probabilistic and not deterministic, to anyone within the universe, at least).

This means that we can retry as many times as we want until some astronomically unlikely improbability occurs in the region of an SRD network, making a probabilistic SRD (P-SRD) which can cause distortions orders of magnitude greater than the component SRDs that it’s composed of.

Warning: P-SRD reload ticks

Running a P-SRD is instantaneous to us, but to the simulating computer, it causes several reloaded frames. Running a P-SRD with a very unlikely condition can easily reach up to the order of 1010010100 ticks, and constructing complicated systems with multiple P-SRDs connected in certain ways can be multiplicative in tick cost, due to basic probability multiplication.

As far as we can tell, the alien computer simulating this universe runs so quickly that they haven’t even noticed when the simulation pauses for an absurd number of ticks, but the longer we pause, the more likely it is that on their timescale the freezing will become noticeable, and the aliens will do something about it. Even if they don’t, we might end up making a P-SRD running so many ticks that the simulating computer never finishes its calculation before the simulation breaks down, effectively pausing time forever.

If we play with the universe too much, we might just destroy the universe.

This leads us to our next glitch.

glitch 4: black holes and spacehack

By setting the range of a P-SRD to an extremely small region, and setting a condition on gravitational waves detected inside, we can set a lower bound on the number of wavefunction collapses that have to occur within the region before terminating the loop, meaning that from our perspective, a large amount of matter teleports into a small region.

Long story short, we can make black holes.

If storing energy was hard enough, storing black holes is worse. All the black holes we intially made ended up exploding (via Hawking radiation), but eventually we worked out that we can keep running P-SRDs to continually generate distortion in the fixed area, as well as P-SRDs along a boundary of where we want the gravitational field to terminate, cancelling out excess gravity by running probabilistic methods in reverse.

With enough artificial black holes and boundary P-SRDs, it’s possible to warp spacetime enough to store large objects in a smaller amount of space, cancelling the leaking effects using a huge system of P-SRDs. At the time of designing this tech, however, it was too dangerous to make massive spacehack systems, or at least until glitch 6.

glitch 5: infinite energy storage

Storing energy is now easy – we take our energy spheres, make some black holes, and release the energy into the black hole. We can increase or decrease (and hence extract) energy stored via artificial Hawking radiation – we set the condition on the P-SRDs to be generation of virtual particles.

With all this infinite energy generation storage in black holes, we figured that further research would probably get us killed in an accident sooner rather than later. We halted study for a month or so while we worked out a way to conduct things safely. But rather than improving safety technology, someone had the bright idea of just going back any time things went wrong. Which brings us to…

glitch 6: SRD loops and perma-saving

Remember the basic SRD, from all the way back, capable of making the tiniest reloads via particle displacement?

In order to make a long term save, we made a loop of SRDs, each with a system of particles that is “saturated” to the point that any small interference would cause two particles to be too close together, causing an instant reload, which via our SRDs get pushed apart, sending us a tick into the past. However, the particle that just got pushed away now overlaps with particles in the previous SRD, repeatedly reversing ticks in the loop until we reach the point when the SRD loop is activated.

By slightly modifying the SRD loop, it’s possible to make a more complicated system of loops in which information can be sent back into the past every time the save is reloaded, by causing particle proximity errors to occur in an ordered way, preserving a message.

An important consequence of a perma-save SRD loop is that it acts almost like a checkpoint in a game – once you save at a checkpoint, all the previous checkpoints are overwritten, and you can never get back to a time before the last SRD loop made.

We used to have a few SRD loops which get started up before doing anything potentially dangerous, as well as weekly saves, fitted with detectors and information storage which automatically reload the universe should they get destroyed (as well as telling us how many times they’ve been used).

As a general rule, whenever we researched some things, we made a plan on how to do it, and then only proceeded if the SRD loop counter hasn’t exceeded a certain number. If the number was too high, it meant we’ve tried too many times and it hasn’t worked, in which we re-think our plan.

Saving too often is a bad idea, though – if something goes terribly wrong, then the further back we’re sent, the more time we have to prepare.

We thought that checkpoints couldn’t be overwritten for a long time, but turns out there’s actually a way to do so.

Loops of loops

By making a sequence of loops in which the actual activation of a loop is dependent on information sent from a future reload, it’s actually possible to make multiple nested checkpoint saves, in which reloading to multiple different times is possible.

We have a massive SRD-loop which stores a new save daily. If it gets damaged, the loop reload occurs in a matter of ticks, sending us back a day by default. There were some instances where we had a week-long experiment, which was doomed to fail and destroy the entire laboratory – in these cases, if the counter exceeded something like 100, we just loaded to a week ago, sending all the data we’d obtained throughout the six days leading up to the experiment failure back to the start point.

glitch 7: ranged P-SRDs and the universal constructor

Given a sufficiently complicated P-SRD loop, it’s possible to artificially create non-physical P-SRDs, by inducing probabilistic electrical or gravitational fields that collapse under a certain condition. Using this, it’s possible to actually make a self-replicating virtual P-SRD (V-SRDs), which creates new versions of itself in each reset, making a chain of V-SRDs that can extend to induce a condition on a given position in space, after which all the virtually induced SRDs dissipate naturally.

With this, it’s possible to use the same “teleportation” trick with V-SRDs to construct objects from a distance outwards, like a 3D-printer working from the inside out, except that the object gets printed almost instantly, on the scale of billionths of billionths of a nanosecond.

This means that given data on how to construct an object, we could theoretically construct it using a hypothetical “universal constructor”.

Of course, actually making one would take an impossibly long time to research.

So that’s what we did.

In each iteration, we’d research something different, collect the data, and then send it back to the main timeline, with a reset-length of a month. We ran about a thousand iterations, collecting years of research into a single day. After a while, we had to increase the reset-length since it took longer for us to actually process and learn what we’d previously(?) discovered.

But eventually, we got there.

We had a machine which could assemble any physical object given its design instantly. Of course, this machine was massive, so we had to use spacehack to actually contain it.

This is the point where our technological advancement exploded.

We’d run huge amounts of resets, each developing a new technology, except this time we could actually print off this technology back at the beginning of each loop. We’d create new tech, and use it to create even more complicated designs. We’d collect decades of scientific progress in a single week.

The list of things we’ve made now is huge, and information/designs are stored in a massive database. Teleportation and faster-than-light-spacecraft are only the tip of the iceberg.

Of course, the rest of the world has no idea that our tech is hundreds of years ahead. Not that anyone would believe us if we didn’t want them to, of course.

Addendum (17/03/2021, 57th period, 478754th iteration):

Something arrived in the constructor message system today.

It’s P-SRD tracking tech, with a rudimentary counter. Apparently it’s set for the end of 2022. It seems like normal tech we had a while ago, although strangely it’s got no tracking signature, like the usual one we use to send things back.

At first glance, I noted that the counter wasn’t 0. Obviously something had gone wrong in the lab a few times; usual reset procedure.

But then I had a closer look.

It’s been reset 175,325,635,032,891 times.
