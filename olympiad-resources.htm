olympiad resources

This page is still under construction, but to be nice we’ve released it as a beta version. Expect more stuff to appear over time.

If you wanted to look for resources on hdigh, try this ultimate guide . The rest of this page (the one you’re looking at now), however, are links to olympiad resources outside hdigh which I’ve found are really good. Finally, there are some links to other people’s resources if you don’t find these sufficient. If you need more combinatorics study, say, and you can’t find anything suitable here, scroll down to the bottom and see whether other people have anything you need.

This post uses some shorthand notation, which is explained just below. Probably worth checking out, if you wanna know what things mean.

Notation:

  • w[N] means that a post has N worked examples, perhaps rounded off to a nice number. For instance, w10 means 10 worked examples. Usually rounded off to a nice number.
  • q[N] means the post has N questions for the reader. Basically how may exercises you have to do. Also rounded off.
  • p[N] denotes the number of pages. Almost always rounded off.
  • download means the link takes you to an instant download for the file, rather than an online version of the file. Just so’s you know.
  • * means (in my opinion) an exceptional resource. More stars is better.

general

Mathematics Olympiads Discord Server a discord server dedicated to… yeah, maths olympiads. Joining does require a discord account, but one of the perks is that you get to meet many like-minded individuals who are enthusiastic about olympiad maths. There are also mock contests every month to practise on, a daily olympiad question, and an active (at the time of writing) community which means that if you have any queries or concerns you can just go there and ask.

AOPS’ how to write a solution this doesn’t teach you what a proof is. What it does is teach you how to write a proof nicely, making it easy to mark, and ensuring you don’t lose marks due to the proof being unclear, and similar mistakes

The IMO Compendium is a goldmine. It’s every question ever proposed to the imo up until ’92– longlists, not shortlists, and then all the shortlists up to ’04. It’s just brilliant.

AOPS ISL where would I be without this? Shortlisted problems for every IMO year (not sure how far back it goes before they stopped adding the complete shortlist), updated live as soon as the question embargo is lifted. I’ve stolen countless questions from here, although I try to vary up my sources.

The Putnam Archive is a bit different to olympiad maths. It’s aimed towards graduate and undergraduate level university students, but is good for training general mathematical reasoning abilities. I remember at one point I took a week off intensely studying mathematics, and naturally ended up doing mathematics — of a different flavour. The Putnam contests were different enough from Olympiad-style competitions that I still felt like I was taking a reprieve, despite using the same reasoning skills.

algebra

Mildorf’s inequalities hands down one of the best inequalities tutorials out there. A few pages of theory, 30 pages of over w40 fully worked examples, and q30 questions at the end. If you want to get good at inequalities, this guide is your friend. Be warned, it is roughly 2* at the start but progressively gets harder and harder and harder.

Inequalities as explained by evan chen, a nice showcase of powerful inequality methods. Worth reading after getting the very basics of inequalities.

Functional equations in mathematical competitions download This pdf contains most(?) (q92, to be exact) functional equations appearing in contests between 2012 to 2015ish. I still have fond memories of opening up this pdf and slowly working through question after question from here, and this document formed the basis of most of my functional equation skills.

Functional equations and how to solve them is a very detailed p130-ish page complete walkthrough for solving functional equations, starting from scratch. However, it deviates away from the pure-olympiad mathematics into more real-world maths.

Radovanovic functional equations is to functional equations what mildorf’s inequalities is to inequalities. It systematically goes through w25 questions, then hits you with q50 more; which is just brilliant. As you might have gathered, I’m a bit of a fan of functional equations, and Radovanovic’s sheet does not disappoint.

combinatorics

Colourings in grids is another of Yufei Zhao’s worksheets. It leans a lot more on the algebraic side (algebraic side of c-colourings?!) than the colouring side. Short and sweet, like this review: w5q19

“Combinatorics” If you’re more a fan of the comprehensive textbook style approach to things (as opposed to the worksheets here-and-there) then this is probably the book for you. Perhaps not for absolute beginners to combinatorics, but it should be accessible to people who are familiar with basic algebra and counting ideas. q100p70

geometry

Miquel point of cyclic quadrilateral Yufei Zhao’s handout on the complete diagram involving the miquel point of a cyclic quadrilateral. It’s quite a powerful series of lemmas, themselves only occasionally useful, but the proofs and exercises are definitely well worth it for training geometry skills.

Euclidean Geometry for Mathematical Olympiads. I won’t post a permalink for this, because I’m not sure if this is even legal. But this is basically the book for olympiad geometry. Like, it’s that good. It’s like 300 pages last I checked, but in my opinion being familiar with even just the first 70 pages is enough to get you to (and quite well at) international level.

number theory

Cyclotomics download an unrivaled text in cyclotomics. Only p20, but it’s so densely packed that it feels like a lot more. Be warned though, cyclotomics are one of the harder areas in number theory.

Complete number theory …can any text purport to be a complete guide to anything? Well, if anything, this one can. It speeds you through a huge variety of number theory in p23 whilst being q100

SatoNT If you know this guy (and I don’t) then uh.. anyways, p45 and I couldn’t be stuffed counting exercises because they don’t number them in order. Sorry. This is basically “complete number theory” but covers all of that more comprehensively.

mish mash

This here pdf has what appears to be almost a thousand problems from regional level competitions, and it seems like roughly half of them are worked examples. Regional level olympiads are an excellent way to quickly level up olympiad skills after you learn the basics — and when I say after you learn the basics, I mean methods of proof is pretty much all you need before you can hit the ground running.

other people’s resources

*Evan Chen’s page on olympiad maths. Basically outlines a lot of olympiad sheets that he’s made, all of which are very high quality. If you haven’t seen this already, it’s definitely worth taking a look around.

Geoff Smith’s advice on maths olympiads in general.

Po-Shen Loh’s “maths olympiad teaching notes” — last updated like over 5 years ago. Still, there are tonnes of very high level handouts there, you know, cos they coach one of the arguably best teams for the IMO in the world. Most of these problems are not to be taken lightly.

Dominic Yeo, last I checked the UK team’s deputy leader, blog on maths in general. Not specifically targeted towards maths olympiads per se, but you can filter stuff yeah man why can’t you filter stuff on hdigh? Not cool to, you know, find what you want.
