very basic geometry

Let’s dive straight in.

…or not. Quick note, I’m really sorry about inconsistent formatting across different olympiad tutorial posts. With “really sorry” meaning “adherence to social standards” and not, like, y’know, me being actually sorry.

Requisite knowledge (look these up if you don’t know them!) (yeah let’s use degrees cos no trig):

  • Basic algebra (addition and subtraction)
  • Angles on a line add to 180
  • Vertically opposite angles
  • Angles in an isosceles triangle are equal
  • Definitions of shapes (circles, squares, rectangles, triangles, etc)
  • Complementary, supplementary, co-interior angles
  • Tangents equal in length

tl;dr high school maths

definition: angle chasing
Angle chasing is writing down some angles, and using angle deduction rules (requisite knowledge and lemmas) to find other angles.

I’ll draw the diagrams and assume you can just see when lines are parallel or something.

lemma 1
The angle from the center of a circle is always twice the angle from the outside.

∠AOB=2∠APB=2∠AQB∠AOB=2∠APB=2∠AQB

proof: Draw OPOP and notice that OP=OA=OBOP=OA=OB. Label angles and angle chase the isosceles triangles formed by OP,OA,OBOP,OA,OB to finish.

corollary 1.1
Two angles inside a circle to the same side are equal; ABPQABPQ are cyclic iff ∠APB=∠AQB∠APB=∠AQB

lemma 2
Two angles inside a circle on opposite sides add to 180 degrees

proof: use lemma 1 twice (use a reflex angle in one application)

corollary 2.1
ARBQARBQ are cyclic iff ∠ARB+∠AQB=180∠ARB+∠AQB=180

I hope I’m not boring you with the proofs. Also the proofs are left very deliberately vague, cos I’m assuming you kinda know how to do these things, but if you don’t, here’s a walkthrough.

We have a semicircle, the center of the semicircle, and a random point on the semicircle. Prove that the angle is 90 degrees (I hope you know which one I mean).

How do we prove this?

Let’s do some “angle chasing”.

See the blue angles? They have to be equal cos it’s an isosceles triangle. Why is it isosceles? The sides are radii of a circle, and a circle has equal radii. Let’s call the blue angles xx.

Similarly, the orange parts are equal. Let’s call them yy.

Then the red angle has to be 180−2y180−2y because of angle sum in a triangle, and the green angle has to be 180−2x180−2x for a similar reason. But by angles on a straight line, green and red sum to 180 so

180−2x+180−2y=180⟹x+y=90180−2x+180−2y=180⟹x+y=90

And now we’re done, as x+yx+y is the top angle, which is what we were trying to prove was a right angle in the first place.

corollary 1.2 An angle in a semicircle is 90 degrees.

(can you see why this is a corollary of lemma 1?)

Anyways, if you haven’t already, go back to the first few lemmas and properly prove them, not a half-baked effort like I did.

Leave the corollaries alone for now. All they tell you is that if you have points as in the diagram and you know the angles match up, then you can draw a circle through those four points. Useful right now? Very much so. Finding so-called “cyclic quadrilaterals” is super duper critical at increasing your geometry level. Basically every question at this level requires finding and using cyclic quadrilaterals.

Time for some more lemmas.

Don’t get freaked out before we do anything. It’s just lines, that’s all.

lemma 3 The angle bisectors of each of the three vertices intersect in one point, say I (called the incenter).

proof: Consider what each angle bisector is: it’s the set of points which are the same distance from a pair of lines. Thus the angle bisectors at A at B intersect at a point which is the same distance from AB and AC, and the same distance from BA and BC. Thus the distance from this intersection to CA and CB must be equal and thus the angle bisector at C goes through this point.

lemma 4 The perpendicular bisectors of each of the sides of a triangle go through a point, say O (called the circumcenter).

proof: Exercise for the reader. (hint: same as above, except use vertices instead of sides).

lemma 5 The perpendiculars from each vertex to the opposite side are concurrent, say at H (called the orthocenter).

proof: again, consider the intersection of two of them, and show that it lies on the third. This one requires a lot more work so I’ll run you through it properly at the bottom.

lemma 6 The lines joining each vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side are concurrent, say at G (called the centroid).

proof: prove that these three lines cut the triangle into six regions of equal area. Basically just look at areas.

bonus lemma H,G,O are collinear in that order with ratio 2:1

proof: good luck for now, this is really a bonus exercise.

Okay, time for a full proof of lemma 5.

Firstly, notice that ∠BEC=∠BDC∠BEC=∠BDC so BEDC lie on a circle. Similarly, AEHD form a cyclic quadrilateral. Finally, notice that

∠EAH=∠EDH=∠EDB=∠ECB=90−∠CBE∠EAH=∠EDH=∠EDB=∠ECB=90−∠CBE
(all via the aforementioned cyclic quadrilaterals) and thus AH⊥BCAH⊥BC, so the perpendicular from A to BC passes through H as required.

questions
  1. In the last diagram, if you extend AH down to hit BC at F, then out of ABCDEFH, find 6 cyclic quadrilaterals.
  2. In the last diagram, if you reflect H over BC to get P, prove that ABPC are cyclic.
  3. In the last diagram, find as many similar triangles as you can (only using the drawn lines as edges). There are a lot!
  4. Prove that for any non-degenerate triangle (i.e not “flat”), there is a unique circle that passes through all three of its vertices.
  5. Given such a triangle, the circle that passes through all three vertices is called its circumcircle. In the last diagram, prove that the radius of the circumcircle of ABC and ABH are the same. As a bonus, prove that A is the orthocenter of BCH.
  6. In the second-last diagram, what is the relationship between O and DEF? G and DEF?
  7. Let ABCABC be a triangle and DEFDEF points on sides BC,CA,ABBC,CA,AB respectively. Let’s assume PP is a point inside the triangle such that AEFPAEFP, BFDPBFDP are cyclic. Prove that CABPCABP is too.
  8. Circles Ω1Ω1 and Ω2Ω2 intersect in two points, AA and BB. A line through AA intersects Ω1Ω1 at PP and Ω2Ω2 at XX, and another line through AA intersects Ω1Ω1 at QQ and Ω2Ω2 at YY. Prove BPQBPQ and BXYBXY are similar triangles.
  9. (terminology test) If ABCABC is a triangle with incenter II, prove that the circumcenter of BICBIC lies on the circumcircle of ABCABC.
  10. Does there exist a non-equilateral triangle such that at least two of the orthocenter, circumcenter, incenter and centroid are the same point?
