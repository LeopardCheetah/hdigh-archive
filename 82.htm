constructions

Sometimes, adding in the right point in a geometry diagram can make the problem much easier. Unfortunately, knowing which point to add is hard, and if you just add any random point, it just makes your diagram more cluttered. Knowing what to construct is a combination of experience and intuition. With that said, let’s get into some useful constructions!

The easiest constructions are when there’s an obvious intersection of two objects that hasn’t yet been added in. But sometimes, that’s not enough. Keep on the lookout if you see one of the following:

Midpoints

When you see midpoints, there are a few constructions that might prove useful:

  • Parallelograms
    If you have a midpoint MM of some segment ACAC and some other point BB not on the line, it can be useful to construct DD such that ABCDABCD is a parallelogram, because you now get that MM is the midpoint of BDBD for free. The reverse also holds.
  • Right angles
    If MM is the midpoint of ACAC and ∠ABC=90∘∠ABC=90∘, then MA=MB=MCMA=MB=MC (because it’s the centre of circle ABCABC.)
  • Midpoint theorem
    If you’re given the midpoint MM of ABAB in triangle ABCABC, it might also be useful to add in the midpoint NN of side ACAC, because now MN∥BCMN∥BC. If you have midpoints of all three sides, you get a triangle similar to the original (it’s in fact the dilation of ABCABC with factor −12−12 around the centroid GG, a fact which instantly proves the Euler line.)

Circles ω1ω1 and ω2ω2 are externally tangent to each other at XX. AA is on ω1ω1 and BB is on ω2ω2 such that ABAB is tangent to both circles. Prove ∠AXB=90∘∠AXB=90∘.

To do this, consider the tangent at XX, and suppose it intersects ABAB at MM. Since tangents to a point are equal, MA=MX=MBMA=MX=MB, and from the right angle thing we’re instantly done.

(there is a way to do it without constructing the tangent and intersection; see if you can find it!)

Angle bisectors
  • Incentre-Excentre lemma
    In triangle ABCABC, suppose the angle bisector intersects the circumcircle at XX. Then XX is the centre of circle BICIaBICIa (where II and IaIa are the incentre and A-bisector). Constructing said point XX is often really useful. Exercise: prove this fact
  • Incentre in general
    If you have two angle bisectors, you know they intersect at the incentre, so you get a third angle bisector for free.
Spiral centre

If A,B,CA,B,C are collinear and A,D,EA,D,E are collinear, let XX be the intersection of the circumcircles of ABDABD and ACEACE. XX is a useful point to construct because you get △XBC∼△XDE△XBC∼△XDE, and △XBD∼△XCE△XBD∼△XCE.

Less straightforward constructions (e.g. reflecting, rotating, or just anything else in general)

Sometimes you just have to be clever to find the right construction. Being able to do this takes the most creativity and ingenuity, and the right point or line or circle to add might not be obvious at first.

Point DD lies on side BCBC of triangle ABCABC such that ADAD bisects ∠A∠A. If AB+BD=ACAB+BD=AC, prove that ∠B=2∠C∠B=2∠C.

Reflect BB over ADAD to B′B′. Since ADAD is the angle bisector, B′B′ lies on ACAC. Also, AB=AB′AB=AB′ and BD=B′DBD=B′D. So AB′+B′D=ACAB′+B′D=AC, which upon subtracting AB′AB′ gives B′D=B′CB′D=B′C. So DB′CDB′C is isosceles, and so ∠AB′D=2∠B′CD∠AB′D=2∠B′CD. But the former is just ∠B∠B, and the latter is just ∠C∠C.

Problems
  1. ABCDABCD is a square, and a circle with centre OO is inscribed in the square. The circle touches ABAB and CDCD at EE and FF, and ECEC intersects the circle again at XX. The midpoint of EXEX is PP. Find ∠EPF∠EPF.
  2. Prove geometrically that cos36∘+sin18∘=12cos⁡36∘+sin⁡18∘=12.
  3. Given two nonintersecting circles, prove the midpoints of all common tangents are collinear.
  4. Let XX be a point inside parallelogram ABCDABCD. Prove that ∠ABX=∠ADX∠ABX=∠ADX if and only if ∠BAX=∠BCX∠BAX=∠BCX.
  5. ABCABC has circumcentre OO and orthocentre HH. ADAD is an altitutde of ABCABC, and EE is on BCBC such that AE=EOAE=EO. Prove that the circumcircle of ADEADE passes through the midpoint of OHOH.
  6. Acute triangle ABCABC has orthocentre HH, and GG is the point such that ABGHABGH is a parallelogram. II lies on line GHGH such that ACAC bisects HIHI. Line ACAC meets the circumcircle of GCIGCI at CC and JJ. Prove IJ=AHIJ=AH.
  7. In a cyclic quadrilateral ABCDABCD (with points in that order), prove that AB×CD+BC×AD=AC×BDAB×CD+BC×AD=AC×BD.
  8. Let ABCABC be an acute triangle with orthocenter HH, and let WW be a point on the side BCBC, lying strictly between BB and CC. The points MM and NN are the feet of the altitudes from BB and CC, respectively. Denote by ω1ω1 is the circumcircle of BWNBWN, and let XX be the point on ω1ω1 such that WXWX is a diameter of ω1ω1. Analogously, denote by ω2ω2 the circumcircle of triangle CWMCWM, and let YY be the point such that WYWY is a diameter of ω2ω2. Prove that X,YX,Y and HH are collinear.
  9. Convex quadrilateral ABCDABCD satisfies AB⋅CD=BC⋅ADAB⋅CD=BC⋅AD, and XX lies inside ABCDABCD such that ∠XAB=∠XCD∠XAB=∠XCD and ∠XBC=∠XDA∠XBC=∠XDA. Prove that ∠BXA+∠DXC=180∘∠BXA+∠DXC=180∘.
  10. ABCABC is a triangle with incentre II. Point PP satisfies AP+BC=BP+CA=CP+ABAP+BC=BP+CA=CP+AB, and point QQ satisfies AQ−BC=BQ−CA=CQ−ABAQ−BC=BQ−CA=CQ−AB. Prove that P,I,QP,I,Q are collinear.
