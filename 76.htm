reverse reconstruction and basic geoguessr

–also known affectionately as phantom points.

In geometry, suppose you wanna prove something about a point PP. Say, you wanna prove P,Q,RP,Q,R are collinear. But that seems really hard.

What you could do instead is construct P′P′ such that P′,Q,RP′,Q,R are collinear, and then prove that P′=PP′=P.

This is all a bit silly right now so let’s do an example.

I’d do the classic equilateral triangle in a square (see problem 1), but that’s kinda overused so let’s prove the converse of pythagoras instead:

Pythagoras’ Theorem

A triangle is right angled iff there exists a labelling of the sides a,b,ca,b,c such that

a2+b2=c2a2+b2=c2

If a triangle is right angled, then this theorem is not hard to prove at all. Give it a shot! You can do it with similar triangles, or constructing squares, or whatever. It’s hard to avoid the teensiest bit of algebra though — hard but not impossible. See if you can manage.

Anyways, see the iff in the middle there? That means that if a triangle has a2+b2=c2a2+b2=c2, we want to prove that the triangle is right angled.

What do you do? Well, consider a right angled triangle with legs of length aa and bb. The hypotenuse must have length c′c′ with c′2=a2+b2c′2=a2+b2, so c′=cc′=c. But by SSS congruence, the considered triangle and the original triangle must be congruent, and so the original triangle is right angled.

Consider the above argument very carefully. We never proved anything directly about the original diagram — rather, we took another diagram where the points satisfied the end goal (right angled) and proved that it matched the original diagram.

Note 1: Note that proving that the end goal matches the original diagram is very important — it’s possible for you to provide a diagram that works but you forget to prove that it’s the only one that does, akin to proving that “all squares of even numbers are even” by consider 44.

Note 2: Another thing to notice is that proofs of triangle centers are usually reverse reconstruction in disguise. For instance, the proof that the orthocenter exists constructs AHAH and shows that it’s perpendicular to BCBC.

Note 3: Why did I throw geoguessr in the title? Because for a few of these questions, you wanna guess an alternate characterisation for a point and then prove it satisfies the prerequisites. More advanced geoguessr techniques will be required soon!

Note 4: Anyways, here are the problems! Bonus points if you do any of the questions without reverse reconstruction.

Note 5: Oh, and one final note — virtually all iff theorems have one direction that can be proved by reverse reconstruction.

Note 6: Okay, so I added the note numbering after I realised how many “last things” I had to say, and yeah, it’s a lot isn’t it. But hey, at least they’re numbered and labelled as notes now so you know they’re not actually important.

questions
  1. Suppose that PP lies inside square ABCDABCD such that ∠PAB=∠PBA=15∘∠PAB=∠PBA=15∘. Prove that PCDPCD is equilateral.
  2. In a triangle ABCABC with incenter II, prove that the circumcenter of ABIABI lies on the circumcircle of ABCABC.
  3. Prove the converse of Ceva’s and Menalaus’ Theorems: If A′,B′,C′A′,B′,C′ lie on lines BC,CA,ABBC,CA,AB of triangle ABCABC respectively, then consider (using directed lengths)
    k=AB′B′CCA′A′BBC′C′A.k=AB′B′CCA′A′BBC′C′A.

    AA′,BB′,CC′AA′,BB′,CC′ are concurrent iff k=1,k=1,
    A′,B′,C′A′,B′,C′ are collinear iff k=−1.k=−1.
  4. ABCABC is a triangle and DD lies on the bisector of ∠A∠A such that ∠BAD+∠BDC=90∘∠BAD+∠BDC=90∘. In terms of the angles of ABCABC, find ∠ADB∠ADB.
  5. On circle ABCABC, the midpoint of arc BCBC not containing AA is A1A1 and the midpoint of segment BCBC is A2A2. B1,B2,C1,C2B1,B2,C1,C2 are defined similarly. Prove that A1A2,B1B2,C1C2A1A2,B1B2,C1C2 are concurrent.
  6. Suppose that in a triangle, there exist three lines which each split both the perimeter and area in half. Prove that these lines are concurrent.
  7. Let ω1,ω2,Γω1,ω2,Γ be three mutually externally tangent circles. Let the tangent to ω1ω1 and ΓΓ not passing through ω2ω2 be ℓ1ℓ1, and define ℓ2ℓ2 similarly. ℓ1ℓ1 intersects ω1ω1 at PP, and ω2ω2 intersects ΓΓ at QQ. Suppose that ℓ1ℓ1 and ℓ2ℓ2 are parallel. Prove that PQPQ is tangent to ΓΓ.
  8. Suppose that ABCDABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral with AB+CD=ADAB+CD=AD. Prove that the angle bisectors at ∠B∠B and ∠C∠C intersect on ADAD.
  9. Consider all six angle trisectors inside ABCABC. Suppose that the two going through AA and BB closest to ABAB intersect at C′C′. Define A′A′ and B′B′ similarly. Prove that A′B′C′A′B′C′ is equilateral.
  10. Let II be the incentre of acute triangle ABCABC with AB≠ACAB≠AC. The incircle ωω of ABCABC is tangent to sides BC,CABC,CA, and ABAB at D,E,D,E, and FF, respectively. The line through DD perpendicular to EFEF meets ωω at RR. Line ARAR meets ωω again at PP. The circumcircles of triangle PCEPCE and PBFPBF meet again at QQ. Prove that the lines DIDI and PQPQ meet on the line through AA perpendicular to AIAI.
