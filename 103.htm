self-interest, laziness, and the end of the world

You may or may not have heard of something called the prisoner’s dilemma. If you haven’t, here’s the situation. Two people who have committed a crime have got arrested, and are separately contained, so they can’t talk to each other. They both get questioned individually about the crime. Each person has two options – they can either rat the other person out, or stay silent. Depending on who rats who out, the outcomes are different:

  • If they both betray each other, they both get 5 years in jail.
  • If person A betrays person B, but not vice versa, A goes free while B gets 10 years. (If B betrays A but not vice versa, the situation is reversed.)
  • If nobody betrays anyone, they both get 6 months.

Now should A betray B, or not? For person A, the decision has two parts. If B betrays A, it’s better for A to betray B, because A will get 5 years instead of 10. If B doesn’t betray A, then it’s better for A to betray B, because being free is better than 6 months.

So either way, A is better off if they betray B, and similarly, B should betray A, right? But if both of them took the illogical decision, they’d both end up with less time in prison than if both were logical. So what’s going on here?

This is a famous example of how everyone acting in self-interest is worse than everyone acting for the good of the whole. But the issue is, we can’t make everyone do good for everyone else – people have the option to cheat and be better off than the others. And so it comes down to this:

No matter what you do, some people will always be selfish.

From the perspective of these people, there isn’t really a reason not to betray someone else, as long as they’re better off for it. The degrees of this vary wildly – some people are extremely ruthless when it comes to self-gain, while most others are less extreme. Everyone stands at different levels, although i’m sure most people agree that being too self-centred is bad.

But unfortunately, the very slightest bit of self-interest is about to doom the world.

Let’s take a look at the prisoner’s dilemma again, except instead of two people, we have, say, 7.7 billion. Each person has a choice, which causes reasonable effort and cost, but which benefits everyone – except it’s divided up among billions of people. For most people, doing taking this choice is unreasonable, because it makes statistically insignificant difference to the world anyways.

This choice is acting against climate change.

It’s true – one person deciding to recycle is less than millionths of a percent towards solving the problem. To the average person, changing power sources to solar panels, or not using a car, is far too much cost to justify this tiny change. And despite some attempts to raise awareness, this rationality is just too strong for most of the world population, for many reasons.

Personal effect vs global effect

Most humans do things on an inherently personal level – for example, a minor inconvenience, like tripping over, is far more damaging to someone’s day than hearing that a natural disaster happened on the other side of the world. Climate change is a hugely global issue – it happens over the course of years, and can’t significantly be influenced by the personal effort of one person. The more likely outcome is that a significant proportion people won’t make a move unless something adverse happens to them all – but any outcome that already affects most of the population is probably large enough that by that point it’s too late to reverse. In such an extreme version of the prisoner’s dilemma, people will choose the good of themselves, if the choice is left up to them. It’ll take the influence of larger organisations and governments to do anything – we’ll return to that later.

Short term, long term

Climate change takes many years. People tend to be more concerned about what happens next week than what happens 50 years down the line, and so for most people, climate change isn’t even on their mind. That appointment tomorrow, or the blog post that needs to be finished in a couple of hours, are far more pressing in the minds of most.

Relevance

This one is similar to the previous one – for many people, climate change isn’t the priority. This holds true even for governments and businesses. For the majority of most developed countries, the state of the economy or the next election take up much more attention than global warming, and poorer countries are focusing on feeding their people, if they’re not controlled by corruption. Governments currently spare only small proportions of their GDP towards combatting climate change.

So if individuals won’t make a change, and neither will the people in power, will we actually be able to solve climate change?

Probably not.

Global warming will probably continue, and the global ecosystem will most likely suffer severely.. Humanity will experience issues with food and flooding, although we’ll most likely survive, though definitely not unscathed. It will definitely be a large scale issue, though humans are probably resourceful enough to find solutions to preserve ourselves.

In terms of actually saving the planet, or getting through this with little harm, it’s probably impossible to make everyone in the world come around to tackling climate change, so the only plausible solution would be if some extraordinary technology gets invented.

I guess this post was half an explanation of human behaviour, and half an attempt to encourage people to live a little more eco-friendly, even though it won’t do much. But even if it’s hopeless, it’s our planet, and there’s no other option. We have to try, no matter how impossible it is. As the poet Dylan Thomas puts it:

“Do not go gentle into that good night. Rage, rage against the dying of the light.”
