am-gm

Let’s start with a basic fact:

Lemma square

x2≥0x2≥0 for all real xx, with equality iff x=0x=0.

Proof: trivial (case bash on x>0,x=0,x<0x>0,x=0,x<0)

Let’s do another basic fact:

Arithmetic Mean definition:

The arithmetic mean of a bunch of numbers is the sum of the numbers divided by how many there are.

In other words, it’s the regular “average” that most of you are familiar with. So, for instance, the average of 5,7,2,9,2 is (5+7+2+9+2)/5=5.

Why go to all the effort of giving it a fancy name, “arithmetic mean”?

That’s because there are other types of means too, and one of them is the “geometric mean”. Despite its name, I’ve never used it in geometry.

Okay hold on. Words are hard.

Geometric Mean definition take 2:

The geometric mean of a1,a2,…,ana1,a2,…,an is

a1a2a3…an−−−−−−−−−−√na1a2a3…ann

What’s the intuition for this? Say somebody has a hundred “skill points” and somebody else has one. If you wanted to find somebody who was “roughly in between” the two, you’d normally pick around fifty right?

But that means that the person with a hundred skill points is 2x better than the person with fifty, whereas the person with fifty is 50x better than the person with 1. That doesn’t seem very in-between does it?

The geometric mean of 1 and 100 is 10. This means the top player is 10x better than the middle player, and the middle player is 10x better than the bottom player. Nice and fair.

Most rating systems (e.g elo for chess or something) already incorporate a sense of geometric mean, or say the decibel system, or the pH scale, or Richter scale, or…

Anyways, this is an inequalities tutorial, so let’s do some inequalities: for positive reals a,ba,b, we have

(a−−√−b√)2≥0⟹a+b≥2ab−−√(a−b)2≥0⟹a+b≥2ab

which leads to

Two variable am-gm

For any two positive reals a,ba,b,

a+b2≥ab−−√a+b2≥ab
with equality iff a=ba=b

This tells you that the arithmetic mean of any two variables is greater than or equal to their geometric mean. In fact, it’s always “greater than” unless the two variables are equal. Pretty nifty, right?

Let’s see an example:

Show that |a+1a|≥2|a+1a|≥2 for all non-zero reals aa.

This is equivalent to proving that it’s true for positive reals aa. But that’s easy! Just apply AM-GM on a,1aa,1a and you’re done.

Farmer Brown has 100m of fence with which to make a rectangular paddock. Fortunately for him, a river runs through his backyard so he only needs to fence off three sides of his paddock. What’s the optimal area that he can fence off?

Suppose the length and width are ll and ww. Then we know that l+2w=100l+2w=100 and we wanna maximise lwlw. This calls for AM-GM! Use AM-GM on ll and 2w2w to get:

l+2w2≥2lw−−−√l+2w2≥2lw
and so
1250≥lw1250≥lw

But wait! The astute reader may have noticed that this proof isn’t actually correct.

Sure, it demonstrates that the area Farmer Brown can fence off is at most 12501250 square meters. But does it show that this number is actually attainable? It’s possible in maths to prove that π≤4π≤4 (in fact, give that a shot from definitions! ππ is the circumference divided by the diameter…) but that doesn’t mean π=4π=4. Similarly, we’ve proven that maximal area is at most 12501250 but we haven’t shown 12501250 is possible.

In fact it turns out that…

It is possible, and if you think carefully, the equality only occurs when the two terms you AM-GM are equal, that is, l=2wl=2w.

Let’s do one last demo question, shall we.

Prove that for any positive reals a,ba,b,

4(a2+b2)2≥(a+b)44(a2+b2)2≥(a+b)4

Expand everything out to find that the statement is equivalent to

3a4+2a2b2+3b4≥4a3b+4ab33a4+2a2b2+3b4≥4a3b+4ab3

Now notice that the terms on the RHS are very “powerful” in that the exponents have a large difference. This makes them perfect candidates for AM-GM. But right now, AM-GM on the LHS has to be done very carefully:

=≥≥3a4+2a2b2+3b42a4+2a2b2+2b4+a4+b4(2a4+2a2b2)+(2b4+2a2b2)4a3b+4ab33a4+2a2b2+3b4=2a4+2a2b2+2b4+a4+b4≥(2a4+2a2b2)+(2b4+2a2b2)≥4a3b+4ab3

The AM-GM inequality

For positive reals a1,a2,a3,…,ana1,a2,a3,…,an,

a1+a2+a3+…+ann≥a1a2a3…an−−−−−−−−−−√na1+a2+a3+…+ann≥a1a2a3…ann
with equality if and only if all the aiais are equal.

Proof
This one is kinda hard, but think about recursively using AM-GM on itself. For instance, to get the 4-variable AM-GM, consider using AM-GM on a,ba,b and then on c,dc,d and then on the resulting square root expressions. Do a similar thing to get AM-GM for all powers of 2. Finally, prove that if you have nn variable am-gm then you can get n−1n−1 variable AM-GM, by letting one of the nn variables be the arithmetic mean of the other n−1n−1 variables.

You can also prove it using lagrange multipliers

The most useful case of the above is in three variable AM-GM, i.e

Three variable AM-GM

For any positive reals a,b,ca,b,c,

a+b+c3≥abc−−−√3a+b+c3≥abc3

One last example:

For any three positive reals a,b,ca,b,c, show that

a3+b3+c3≥a2b+b2c+c2aa3+b3+c3≥a2b+b2c+c2a

Oh no! What do we do here?

Just try some AM-GM of course! Three variable AM-GM, but on weird sets:

a3+a3+b33≥a3⋅a3⋅b3−−−−−−−−√3=a2ba3+a3+b33≥a3⋅a3⋅b33=a2b
and the same holds when we permute a,b,ca,b,c:
b3+b3+c33≥b2cb3+b3+c33≥b2c
c3+c3+a33≥c2ac3+c3+a33≥c2a
. Adding all of these up gives the required result, almost as if by magic.

Inequalities generally have a super short magic solution, despite being hard to find.

One last note about AM-GM: find equality cases! If you know precisely when equality holds, then you don’t have to flaff around with equations that don’t actually work. What I mean by this is that you know precisely when equality holds in AM-GM (all variables equal). Use this to your advantage, because if you know when equality holds in the question, you’re done! For instance, in the Farmer Brown question, if you didn’t know how to AM-GM immediately, then playing around with the question might give you the idea that maximal area is achieved with width 25 and length 50. This would give you a hint as to how to AM-GM the variables: trying AM-GM on 2w and l.
