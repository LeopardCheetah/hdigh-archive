polynomials

A polynomial is a thing that can be written in the form

anxn+an−1xn−1+⋯+a2x2+a1x+a0anxn+an−1xn−1+⋯+a2x2+a1x+a0

Basically anything that’s made up out of powers of xx, addition, subtraction, and multiplication. Okay, finitely many uses.

For instance, 1+πx2−3(x2+1)1+πx2−3(x2+1) is a polynomial but x2+2x−2x2+2x−2 isn’t. Because it uses 2x2x which isn’t allowed under the definition (it’s not a power of xx, and can’t be made with addition, subtraction, multiplication). Also, negative powers of xx aren’t allowed — for instance, 1x21x2 or xπxπ aren’t polynomials.

The degree of a polynomial, degPdeg⁡P, is just the highest power of xx (if none exists, degP=−∞deg⁡P=−∞)

For instance, deg(3x2+100x−10)deg⁡(3x2+100x−10) is 2 since x2x2 is the highest power of xx is x2x2 which is xx to the power of two.

The leading term is the non-zero coefficient of the highest power of xx. If none exists then it’s just.. I’m not sure, tbh. If the leading term is 11, the polynomial is monic.

Vieta’s formulae are also an important thing, which comes from expanding the factorisation of a polynomial. You should try it!

For instance, for quadratics, if α,βα,β are roots of x2+ax+bx2+ax+b, then

(x−α)(x−β)=x2−(α+β)x+αβ=x2+ax+b(x−α)(x−β)=x2−(α+β)x+αβ=x2+ax+b
so a=−α−βa=−α−β and b=αβb=αβ.

Conjugate root theorem

The multiplicity of any (complex) root in a real polynomial is the same as that of its conjugate.

Whoops! I didn’t define multiplicity. Crudely speaking, it’s the number of times that it’s a factor.

Another thing to know about: roots of unity. Don’t fret too much though.

Let’s do an example problem.

Find all polynomials such that for all integers nn,

p(n3)=p(n2)p(n)p(n3)=p(n2)p(n)

Firstly, consider p(n3)−p(n2)p(n)≡0p(n3)−p(n2)p(n)≡0. Thus, since the LHS is a polynomial which is 00 for all natural nn, it must be 00 everywhere.

Now suppose pp has some root aa. Then a3a3 is also a root, and so is a9a9, and so on. Thus if |a||a| isn’t 0 or 1 then p is the zero polynomial. The LHS of the above equation has roots {cube roots of roots of p} and the RHS has roots {square roots of roots of p} U {roots of p}. Assuming that there is a root of pp which is not 00 or 11, we notice that these multisets can’t be equal (if you consider eg the root on the left with the smallest polar angle, you notice a contradiction). Thus all roots of pp are 00 or 11. But then we have ei2π3ei2π3 being a factor of the LHS which is bad. Thus p=xkp=xk for some non-negative integer kk.

Another way of solving the original problem is to consider the second-highest-degree term on the RHS. Work out the details!
