humans, aliens, and a silly argument for why we might go extinct

Here, have a hastily drawn paint picture, before I really get into it:

So I’m not sure if you can really tell, but, like, modern civisilisation really has not existed for that long. Like, the entire history of humanity has really only taken off in the last few thousand years, out of a few million years.

And we’ve “accomplished” so, so much more in the last few millennia than the rest of humanity’s existence.

It’s like… idk, a soccer game, and, after a game of no goals, one team scores10 goals in the last ten seconds.

Or Usain Bolt standing at the start line for the first 9.9 seconds of a race and running to the finish line in the last 0.1 seconds.

Like, I’m not normally fond of really dumb analogies, and those are the dumbest analogies around, but they do illustrate a point. That point being that from a cosmological standpoint, we used to be at a standstill, but now we’re soaring upwards in development.

Okay. I know I’ve gone on and on about development, but what do I really mean by development here? Compared to humanity in the past, sure, we could argue that we’re so much more “technologically advanced”, but they could argue that they’re happier. How do we define “development”?

Life expectancy? Wait no turtles aren’t better than us then. Surely we can’t let that happen.

Warfare capabilities? This one is pretty useful I suppose, but could have a few weird hypotheticals involved, and might depend on the skill of the commanders, etc. which makes it less than ideal (e.g could a submarine really get “beaten” by a helicopter? Or more to the point, what strategies would various armies use and how does that affect the situation? More “powerful” armies have lost many times in history to inferior but better-commanded and organised armies).

Anyways, I mean, sure, feel free to use warfare capability as your yardstick. But the most commonly used idea is using energy to measure how far along a civilisation is.

Normally people use the “total energy a civilisation has harvested to productive use” or something as their heuristic for how powerful a civilisation is. There’s something known as the Kardashev scale and it goes like this:

  • Kardashev Type I utilises (all) the power of a planet. So, for instance all of the earth’s geothermal things, solar energy that lands on earth, wind, etc. Not only uses, but has control of. So like, weather conditioning, you know, that sorta stuff.
  • Kardashev Type II utilises (all) the power of a star. Like, all of the sun’s output gets turned into useful energy. Basically has enough power to, say, control orbits of planets inside a system, and to move more planets into the habitable regions.
  • Kardashev Type III utilises (all) the power of a galaxy. So basically somehow… tame a galaxy? This means you have control of all the matter inside a galaxy and can move it at will. Pretty insane stuff.

I found this site to be quite descriptive of stuff if you wanted further reading. As in, higher up or lower down on the scale.

In terms of energy output, humans were roughly at type ~0.7 in 1973, and 0.73 in 2017.

But slow and steady right! We’re on track for having complete control over earth in a hundred years or so. Let’s finish up this section with a quote from the wikipedia article:

Physicist and futurist Michio Kaku suggested that humans may attain Type I status in 100–200 years, Type II status in a few thousand years, and Type III status in 100,000 to a million years.

So… what’s this got to do with aliens?

Good question! And no, not just for a clickbaity title.

Suppose that aliens already exist out there, somewhere. Remember that really long picture? Yeah, the timeline. Aliens must’ve come into existence sometime. And after dawdling along on the early stages of the evolutionary scale (surely nothing comes into existence already intelligent), perhaps for a few billion years, the alien lifeforms reach a state similar to where humans are at now. When does this happen?

Look at all those blank pixels above where “modern humans” come into existence. If alien intelligence was a thing, then it’s overwhelmingly likely that they’d have been around for millions of years. Millions of years is roughly how long it takes to colonise the galaxy.

Since the scale on that timebar is in the millions of years, well, this means that any intelligent aliens have probably colonised everything we can see.

Anyways, that means that if you think back to the Kardashev thingy, that yes, aliens, if they existed and progressed at a rate similar to human development today, would be basically galactic commanders.

Like, not even just one galaxy, but several.

Of course, this argument is flawed. We made several assumptions — we assumed that aliens, if they existed, came into existence a long time before us. This assumption is like saying “hey that guy just rolled a one billion sided die; I’m gonna assume he didn’t get a 1”. That’s a pretty standard assumption (despite being wrong), and out of the ones I’m making, the most plausible (at least in my opinion).

The second assumption is that aliens progress at the same rate as us. Of course, there’s nothing indicating why this should be true or false or anything in between wait what’s between true and false. But even if alien development were ten times slower, the odds are against us. You’d have to make it a few hundreds of times slower to do anything, and although that’s plausible, I also doubt this is the strongest line of attack to the main argument.

The other assumption I made was that intelligent beings wouldn’t voluntarily stop their development.

I mean, that’s a semi-reasonable assumption. Like, why would anybody voluntarily stop progressing?

One reason would be religion, or to put it in extraterrestrial terms, people disliking the idea of advance.

There have been many instances in history, and countless more in literature, of one group or another opposing the halt of technology. The Catholic Church had multiple scientists killed or imprisoned for advancing the idea that the earth went around the sun. Rome was all good around two millenium ago, and a thousand years ahead of its time, but things went awry (feel free to look up the details of why! I’m no historian myself, but as far as I’m aware it was something to do with lots of things). 1984 depicts a bit of a sad dystopia in which all scientific advance has halted, and The Hunger Games for instance definitely alludes to a decline in technology.

Like but these are all stories and fiction and history and stuff right? But the point is, an alien civilisation might just possibly have succumbed to such a fate. No clue how likely such a self-imposed barrier is, but it’s a possibility.

Which leads to the last assumption I made: nothing happened that was outside the alien’s control. For instance, perhaps they just never came into existence. Life could just be that unlikely. Earth is unique in the universe. Maybe life coming into existence is fine, but the universe is a dangerous place and comets and stuff exist and — yeah poor dinosaurs.

Or maybe life -> intelligent life is actually a really big leap. After all, why would nature put more emphasis on brainpower when really it could be focussing on running away speed or something? All those wasted resources…

Perhaps intelligent life is actually quite common, but by this stage they wipe themselves out through other means, for instance, inventing activities that are more than survival, or nuclear war, or any of a thousand other options.

And then who knows. Perhaps there’s some sort of unknowable danger that happens later on in development that has somehow killed all civilisations that reach it. Pretty spooky stuff when you realise that we haven’t reached it yet. Such a thing is known as a “great filter” and is one of several arguments for why we might go extinct very soon.

If intelligent life really is common (say one in a billion stars, out of the two hundred billion or so in the galaxy), then the only seemingly plausible reason for why the galaxy isn’t already in the hands of one really powerful civilisation is that everything that makes it sufficiently far along the technological ladder has gotten itself killed. Nanobots, diseases, nuclear war; there are a lot of dangers.

But anyways, in my personal opinion, of all the assumptions I’ve made for “why haven’t we seen life elsewhere in the universe” (Fermi’s paradox), the most implausible one is that intelligent life out there really does exist.

In any case, maybe the reason the whole galaxy hasn’t been taken over by a single race is simple.

Maybe we really are alone in the universe.
