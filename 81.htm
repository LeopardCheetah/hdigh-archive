invariants

Invariant simply means something that doesn’t change.

Let’s give it a shot.

Can a chessboard that is missing two opposite corner squares be tiled with dominos?

If you don’t get the question, just look it up. It’s quite famous. Anyways, I’d recommend you at least think about it if you don’t know the answer already. Try it out, see if you can work out whether or not it’s possible, and prove it.

Anyways, the answer is no. But how do you prove such a thing?

The classic argument is to say that “well, the two corner squares you removed are the same colour and we’ll assume without loss of generality that we removed two white squares. Thus number of black squares is always greater than the number of white squares, as whenever you place a domino, you remove one of each colour”.

That’s all fine and dandy, but let’s formalise that solution up a little. We’ll need it if we want to have a chance at tackling harder problems.

Let I=NW−NBI=NW−NB be the number of white tiles minus the number of black tiles. Note that |I|=2|I|=2 at the start of the process, and placing a tile decreases both NWNW and NBNB by 1 each. Thus II never changes. But at the end, if we tile the remaining cells of the chessboard, we know that I=0I=0, a contradiction.

Usually explicitly stating what the invariant is gonna be is good. It makes it easier to follow, and it’s also harder to mess things up. Try some questions and see how you go.

questions
  1. You start with the numbers 1,2,3,…,20191,2,3,…,2019 on a whiteboard. An operation consists of erasing two numbers a,ba,b and replacing the two numbers with
    i) a+ba+b. What is the last number left?
    ii) ab+a+bab+a+b. What is the last number left?
    iii) a+b−2a+b−2. What is the last number left?
    iv) 0.6a−0.8b,0.8a+0.6b0.6a−0.8b,0.8a+0.6b (so you write two numbers back on). Prove that there exists an integer MM such that you can never write a number larger than MM.
    Note: each dot point above is a separate question
  2. You have 2019 white marbles and 2019 black marbles inside a bag, and an unlimited supply of each outside the bag. On a move, you draw two marbles from the bag. If they’re the same colour, you put a white marble back in the bag, otherwise you put a black marble back in the bag. What colour is the last marble?
  3. You remove a square from a chessboard and tile the rest of the chessboard with 1×3 trominos. Which square could you have removed?
  4. Start with 2018201920182019. Every second, you sum all of the digits of your number, and this sum becomes your new number. Prove that you’ll eventually reach a 1-digit number, and find it. What happens if you start with 2019201920192019 instead?
  5. Prove that the 15-puzzle is not solvable.
  6. An open necklace can contain rubies, emeralds, and sapphires. At every step we can perform any of the following operations.
    (1) We can replace two consecutive rubies with an emerald and a sapphire, where the emerald is on the left of the sapphire.
    (2) We can replace three consecutive emeralds with a sapphire and a ruby, where the sapphire is on the left of the ruby.
    (3) If we find two consecutive sapphires then we can remove them.
    (4) If we find consecutively and in this order a ruby, an emerald, and a sapphire, then we can remove them.
    Furthermore we can also reverse all of the above operations. For example, by reversing (3) we can put two consecutive sapphires on any position we wish. Initially the necklace has one sapphire (and no other precious stones). Decide, with proof, whether there is a finite sequence of steps such that at the end of this sequence the necklace contains one emerald (and no other precious stones).
  7. There mm stones on an n×nn×n grid, and each square in the grid can have any number of stones piled on it. At any turn, you can choose four squares which form a rectangle with sides parallel to the sides of the grid. You can then take one stone each from opposite corners of the rectangle, and move one each to the other pair of opposite corners. Two ways of piling stones are equivalent if one can be reached from the other after some moves. How many non-equivalent ways can the board start?
  8. Consider a lattice polygon PP, and suppose it can be tiled purely with the green blocks from Tetris. Prove that no matter how it is tiled using green and red blocks, there must be an even number of red blocks.
  9. You start with two rational numbers mnmn and nmnm written on the whiteboard. At any point, you may choose two numbers xx and yy written on the board, and then write x+y2x+y2 and 2xyx+y2xyx+y on the board as well. Find all pairs (m,n)(m,n) such that you can eventually write 11.
  10. Consider a 2018×20192018×2019 board with integers in each unit square. Two unit squares are said to be neighbours if they share a common edge. In each turn, you choose some unit squares. Then for each chosen unit square the average of all its neighbours is calculated. Finally, after these calculations are done, the number in each chosen unit square is replaced by the corresponding average. Is it always possible to make the numbers in all squares become the same after finitely many turns?
