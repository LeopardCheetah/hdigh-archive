to be immortal

If there was one thing that humans fear, it would be death. Not spiders, not heights, not fear itself, but death.

Death is the cessation of consciousness. In other words, when you stop having conscious thought. But we lose consciousness all the time — it’s recommended to do so at least once a day! Why is it that we fear death so much?

I guess I’m being slightly unfair here — it’s not exactly death that we fear, but the idea that one stops existing. For instance, if you believe in some sort of heaven or afterlife, then the thought of death is so much more bearable. The idea of not existing has been commonly compared to the idea that you didn’t exist in the world before you were born. Obviously you don’t (currently) really mind not existing then, so why care about what happens after you die?

Evolution, I suppose. An animal which doesn’t instinctively fear death just gets out-Darwinianed by one which does.

Back to the topic at hand, if we removed the barrier of death from our lives, what would it be like?

Just some quick notes on exactly what I mean: you are actually immortal, living forever in a Greek-gods style way, in whatever indestructible (but human) body you choose at the start.

First stage: the part lots of people worry about

Hey, you’re now immortal! That also means you happen to outlive all your immediate family. Close friends. And literally everybody you know, unless there’s some radical advance in technology.

But no, not just all of your friends now. You also manage to outlive every single friend you’ll ever make, in your infinite lifetime. That’s a lot of friends.

A lot of people are rightfully frightened of immortality because they don’t want to live through the deaths of loved ones, of people they know. It’s a painful process, but one that I guess you’ll have to live through.

Second stage: surviving in society

Society changes.

If you’ve ever wondered why the ahem, “more experienced” members of society seem to have trouble with technology, it’s because of change. Sure, decreased neuroplasticity (the ability of the brain to learn new things) might have a small impact on this. But the main reason is that adapting to change is hard.

Learning something is comparatively easy, because all you have to do is wire your brain, so to speak. However, adapting to change requires rewiring your brain. The time it takes to learn how to use the latest technology is often too much to effort to bother with. Worse, by the time you learn how to use it, you’ll be behind. It’s an ongoing effort.

You’ll sort of have to deal with this a lot.

On the plus side, if technology and things do change that much, you’ll probably end up living in a super cool world, with all sorts of “futuristic” technology that is super fun.

Another aspect to survive would be your immortality secret. Assuming that life-extensions aren’t that great yet (and that you’re unique in your predicament), you’d have to move around a lot, keep switching up pseudonyms, and forge identity documents such as birth certificates, to survive. At most a decade or two in an area before your friends start noticing that you aren’t aging.

If the secret gets out, it’d be pretty hard to not have scientists try to take you apart, or have some uber rich billionaire kidnap you and try and figure out the secret to immortality. At the very least, you’d end up pretty famous with paparazzi following you everywhere for a bit, which is always nice, right? Who doesn’t want to be famous?

Third stage: to the end(?) of humanity… and life

Okay, so firstly you have to somehow not get struck by a major tragedy, including but not limited to:

  • Somehow getting yourself buried alive, or falling into a really deep mineshaft, or something equally silly. You’d have to wait aeons for the geological digestive tract to spit you back out.
  • Getting tired of living, trying to commit suicide by jumping into the sun or something, and being pretty majorly stuffed.
  • Going on a rocket ship, insert accident of some sort, you’re now floating by yourself in the endless void (see stage five)
  • Basically anything that leaves you out of reach of the rest of humanity

It’s not hard to avoid these, but it’d just be really tragic if you got yourself entrapped and missed a lot of cool things. I mean, if there were two things I’d want immortality for, the first would be to experience life to its fullest, and the second would be to see how the rest of human history pans out. If the tragedy is bad enough or human civilisation short-lived enough, getting a major tragedy is pretty unlucky.

But back to humanity dying, obviously this depends on how long it takes. It’s possible that we kill ourselves within a decade, or century, or millennium. We could also stay on earth, or in this solar system, and perish with the sun in a few billion years. The other option is we somehow get out of the solar system and hopefully survive for many billions to trillions of years.

If a doomsday scenario occurred, you’d be stuck by yourself for ages and ages. Me, I’d build a rocket somehow and escape earth. Another idea that I noticed on some forums is to selectively breed another species to intelligence to keep you company. That’d take a few million years (a guess, obviously. I don’t think we have any hard data on this), but so would singlehandedly building a rocket.

Otherwise you’re stuck on earth by yourself, which wouldn’t be so bad but would get dreadfully dull.

I mean, if you’re the type of person who gets bored by yourself on a Sunday afternoon, this probably isn’t for you.

If humanity perishes, there might be other friendly intelligent life out there, and you could become some sort of interstellar (or even intergalactic) starfarer, hopping from one planet to another, being worshiped as a god by some, as a… look, I’m honestly not too sure what aliens do. You could help another alien species succeed where humans failed and help them populate the galaxy. The problem is, you’d obviously be different from them. But if humanity survives that long… we’d have evolved too. Regardless, you’d be an outsider in whatever society you end up in.

Since I’m a bit of a completionist, I should mention that there’s a slim possibility that some lifeform (barring you) survives literally forever. Without some radical shift in physics, the only way I can think of that would allow this to happen would be a multiverse theory where travel between universes is possible. Then humanity branches out through the multiverse, and so you tag along and avoid the heat death of this universe. Probably don’t rely on this happening. If it does indeed happen, then it might actually be fun! But stage five would still happen. Stage five is pretty darn near inevitable.

Fourth stage: death of the universe(?)

The universe as we know it is probably going to be around for a long time.

Even when it does eventually die, it’s not gonna be a sudden “Game over: you survived 14 billion years” type thing, but more of a very gradual, slow death.

Let’s take a look from space.

For millions of years, everything would look the same. Billions of years, and stars are starting to disappear, one by one, from the night sky. Sure, new stars are born once in a long while. But stars are dying much, much faster, and soon the entire night sky is black. The stelliferous era has ended. We’re at roughly a hundred trillion years.

Just to give you an instinctive feeling for how long that is, that’s roughly long enough for you to count up to a quintillion, or 1,000,000,000,000,000,000. In theory at least. In practise, you’d probably make a mistake somewhere because remembering that many digits is hard.

Okay, I’ll skip a bit of stuff that happens along the way to get to some weird physics. Einstein’s theory of gravitation predicted a lot of cool physics results, most of which have been directly observed. But one such conjecture, the existence of “gravity waves”, took almost a century to verify. Einstein himself tried to publish a paper discrediting gravity waves.

The basic idea is that gravity acts sort of like a pond of water, so if something is moving around it creates ripples in the water, which radiate energy away (okay, yes, the object has to be accelerating). So for instance, the earth orbiting the sun — energy is radiated away by these waves, and thus the earth will slowly lose energy. What this means in terms of orbiting is that it will eventually get closer and closer to the sun, ultimately crashing into whatever remains of the sun.

It also means stars in a galaxy will either get flung out, or spiral into the black hole in the center.

Basically at this point, all we have left are a bunch of bodies, each floating independently of everything else. It’s a pretty bleak universe. This isn’t really the end, and it gets worse still, but let’s wrap it up there for now.

Having watched the universe die, you’re either stuck on some asteroid, or floating by yourself in nothingness, or something. The idea is you’ve run out of energy, raw materials, — it’s pitch black and you’ve only got your own thoughts for company.

Fifth stage: the meaning of forever

Forever is a long time.

You know, for me this is the scariest part of living forever. It’s not that you’ll end up stuck in a void forever, or that everybody you know will be dead, or that you’re by yourself. I think I could just about cope with those things.

It’s just that forever is a really long time.

People often talk about this in terms of a really large volume of space: imagine if space went on for a googolplex meters. The possibility space is then so unimaginably large, that it is almost certain there are exact copies of you out there — exact copies of you but with one atom different, exact copies of you but with different hair colour, exact copies of you but you hadn’t wasted all that time writing hypothetical blog posts instead of studying.

Now think about that, but in terms of time.

There are only finitely many thoughts you can have. Sure, this number might be inconceivably large. But at some point, you would have to start repeating thoughts. No, not just repeating thoughts — but long periods of time where you are thinking the exact same things as you did ages ago, over, and over, and over. And you wouldn’t even notice.

You know, according to estimates, the number of ways you can fill a human-sized lump of space with stuff is “only” 101070101070 or so. That means that at some point, you’d be having the exact same thoughts as you-from-unimaginably-large-amount-of-time-years-ago. Eventually, you’d also be the exact same copy as you from a long, long time ago. Exact same memories (not necessarily real memories of things that actually happened), exact same thoughts — everything is the same. Eventually, everything you do, or think, or otherwise interact with will be something you’ve done before. You’ll never have a new experience, or new thought, or new memory.

And not only that, you’ll be stuck in this loop and do it all again, forever and ever.

Oh, of course you’ll think you’re having new thoughts and experiences and ideas. You won’t remember having had them before — your brain can only hold so much, after all. In a strange way, there isn’t really a difference between the first and the millionth time you think of something.

If you’re alone or by yourself at this point — well, the brain can’t handle zero sensory input. You’d eventually dream yourself into some fantasy, something that isn’t real. Given enough time, these delusions would eventually cover every possible scenario. Some of those delusions would be pleasant, some would be utterly horrific. You’d dream yourself through every possible world, story, as every character, alien, and through every possible experience.

Infinity is just that big.

And somewhere in those eternal dreams, you’d live your mortal life again.
