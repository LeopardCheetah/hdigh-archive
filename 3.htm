let’s play a game

Everyone’s played naughts and crosses at one point. If you think about it hard enough, you’ll realise (if you haven’t already) that no matter if you start first or second, you can always guarantee that you won’t lose. If you want proof, google “tic tac toe” and a game will come up; set the mode to impossible and the computer will always win or draw. If you really can’t work out how to always win/draw, there’s even a helpful map just for you.

But most games aren’t that simple; I doubt that you’ve worked out a strategy for how to always win chess every time. Even worse, some games aren’t just complicated, they’re rigged. If you want an example, take the 21 game. Two players take turns counting up from 1 to 21, and each player can say between one to three numbers at a time. The player that says 21 loses. So an example game is like follows:

A: 1, 2
B: 3, 4, 5
A: 6
B: 7, 8, 9
A: 10
B: 11
A: 12, 13, 14
B: 15, 16
A: 17, 18
B: 19, 20
A: 21

So A loses.

At first glance, the game might not seem too biased, but it turns out there’s a simple strategy for the second player to always win; they just need to count up to the nearest multiple of 4. So if A says one number, B will say three more, if A says two, B says two, and so on. This means that B always ends on a multiple of 4, but 20 also happens to be a multiple of 4, so after B says 20, A has no choice but to say 21.

Working out whether games are rigged is usually pretty hard, and working out the strategy to win is even harder. White wins 55% of the time in chess, and there may exist a strategy for them to always win, even though we don’t know that strategy yet. In any case, the strategy would be far too complicated for humans to remember – computers have been testing all possible games and still haven’t finished.

If you have too strong a sense of fairness, rigged games may annoy you. But if you’re a little more mischievous like me, you could use secretly rigged games to win against all of your friends, which will make everyone think either that you’re a genius or an asshole. Some of these games are hilarious because all you have to do is just copy your opponent and still win (and it’s even funnier when they can’t figure out what you’re doing) – so for example misère tic-tac-toe.

The rules are simple: you play normal tic-tac-toe on a 3×3 grid. The first player to make three in a row loses.

Wait, what? Loses? Obviously going first is a major disadvantage. In fact, there’s only one strategy that guarantees a draw. Time for an ad break ~ see if you can come up with a solution while the ad plays.

Okay, so it’s not really an actual ad. I wouldn’t do that to you. What this is is a slightly more rigorous treatment of the topic (i.e rigged is not real terminology). Skip this box if you’re not really the nerdy kinda person.

In game theory, what we’re considering are known as combinatorial games. These games satisfy four basic properties:

  • Turn based: these games are not real time, players take turns
  • Two perfect players: keep it simple with two players, both of whom are trying to win
  • Perfect information: Everybody can see every bit of information
  • No chance: pretty self-explanatory. A card game where you pick up cards like Uno doesn’t qualify, nor do dice games

Game theory tells us an intuitive about these games: if the only possible results are win, loss and draw, then either exactly one of the players can force a win (i.e they can win no matter what the opponent does) or neither player can win and both can force a draw.

Okay, so the strategy is simple: go in the middle first. It’s very counter-intuitive, as going in the middle seems to be the worst possible move! But the rest of the strategy is simple: mirror their moves. If they go top left, you go bottom right.

Amazingly, this is the only strategy that always works! You can challenge other people to the game, and if they go first, it will take them a while to work out the strategy.

Another game where you can copycat, although harder to execute, is the coins game:

Two players take turns placing equally sized coins on a circular table. Coins are not allowed to overlap, and if the table runs out of space and you can’t place any coin on your turn then you lose.

This game looks like it could get complicated after like 100 coins have been placed, but the actual winning strategy is very simple. Player one simply places their first coin in the middle. After this, wherever the second player plays, the first player simply has to play diametrically opposite. This means that after every move, if you rotate the table 180 degrees, it’ll look exactly the same. It works because of the fact that if player two can fit a coin on, player one is guaranteed to to be able to fit one on the opposite side.

Sometimes strategies to win some games are somewhat similar to copying but require a bit of modification. For example, consider the following:

Suppose that we have a row of squares which goes on forever, and two players take turns writing 1 or 2 in an unfilled box (player one can only write 1, and player two… you can probably work out what player two does). If someone writes their number in 3 boxes in a row they win.

If you’re player two, you can ensure that you can never lose by the following: split the row into 1×2 dominoes, and then whenever player one plays, fill the other square of the domino. This means that every filled domino has both a 1 and a 2, so there can never be three 1s in a row because this would require a domino to be filled with 1s. It’s also possible to slightly edit this strategy so that the first player never loses, so if your opponent is smart, the game will go on forever. You’d better use the bathroom before starting.

The last few games have been pretty tame, but here’s where things get really weird. Imagine you have chess, except every player plays two moves on their turn (as if chess wasn’t already hard enough!). Let’s call this “Double chess”. Then there’s a way for white to never lose no matter what! This sounds pretty unbelievable, given that nobody’s solved chess yet. And the thing is — nobody knows a strategy for Double chess, but it’s actually possible to show that this strategy exists, without even describing any details of the strategy at all!

Basically, if this strategy didn’t exist, then this would meas that white can’t avoid losing, so there must be a strategy for black to guarantee a win if they play correctly. Imagine that black wrote this strategy down somewhere, and then white sneaked and looked at this strategy. That means that if white can somehow “pretend” to be black, white can win! Hmm, I wonder. Is there any way for white to reset the board to the starting position after their first “move”?

The answer is yes: white sneakily moves their knight out and back in on their first move. Now the board is back to the beginning, so black is essentially “white” (since chess is symmetric). So white can now use the strategy that they stole, and win! Well, that’s obviously impossible, since we assumed black had a surefire plan. So we know that the strategy for black doesn’t exist, so if white plays properly (whatever that means) then white can always draw or win.

To finish, here are seven fun games to play with other people and always win with! (Probably less fun for your opponents when they keep losing.)

  • Chessboard stepper On an 8×8 chessboard, a counter starts in the lower-left corner. Players take turns moving it one square up, down, left or right, but they can’t visit the same square twice. If a player cannot make a move they lose. If 8×8 is too easy, try 9×9!
  • Split or steal Start with a stack of a hundred (or any reasonable number) coins. On their turn, each player can either choose to split any stack into two stacks (sizes don’t have to be equal), or take one coin from a stack. The player who takes the last coin wins.
  • A colouring game Get a single-width strip of grid paper. Pick a colour each (say blue and red), and take turns colouring uncoloured squares blue or red. The catch is you can’t colour two adjacent cells the same colour. If all squares get filled, it’s a draw. Otherwise, if you run out of options, it’s game over and you lose.

Okay, I’ll be honest, the first two games aren’t actually that fun to play. And yeah, the names aren’t really ‘official’. The next few are though:

  • Hex Take turns colouring grey squares blue or red, on the grid shown below. Player Blue wins if they join the blue edges with a solid blue connected path, otherwise player red wins.
  • Chomp You start with a block of chocolate. On your turn, you pick a square that’s left of chocolate and carve out all of the chocolate above and to the right of the square. Oh yeah, and the bottom left square is poisoned so if you eat it you die. The aim is to avoid dying. Not fun at all.
  • Dots and boxes (modified) Find a dotted square grid, and take turns drawing lines between adjacent pairs of dots. You get to put your name inside a square if you complete the fourth wall (let’s be honest, I’d probably just break it again). Whoever has more names in boxes at the end wins. Note for people who are already experienced with this game, the modification is that you don’t get to make another move if you complete a box.

And last but not least, the game that started it all:

  • Nim Get a few stacks of coins all of different sizes. On each turn, each player can take any number of coins, but they have to take at least one, and they must only take coins from the same pile. If you take the last coin you win!
