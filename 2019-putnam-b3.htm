2019 putnam B3

Let QQ be an nn-by-nn real orthogonal matrix, and let u∈Rnu∈Rn be a unit column vector (that is, uTu=1uTu=1). Let P=I–2uuTP=I–2uuT, where II is the nn-by-nn identity matrix. Show that if 11 is not an eigenvalue of QQ, then 11 is an eigenvalue of PQPQ.

Let MM be a suitable orthogonal matrix such that MuMu is the first standard basis vector e1=⎛⎝⎜⎜⎜⎜10⋮0⎞⎠⎟⎟⎟⎟e1=(10⋮0).

Note that eigenvalues are not affected by conjugation by an invertible matrix, so we let

A=MPM−1=MM−1–2MuuTM−1=I–2MUuTMT=I–2Mu(Mu)T=I–2e1eT1=⎛⎝⎜⎜⎜⎜⎜−11⋱1⎞⎠⎟⎟⎟⎟⎟,A=MPM−1=MM−1–2MuuTM−1=I–2MUuTMT=I–2Mu(Mu)T=I–2e1e1T=(−11⋱1),

and B=MQM−1B=MQM−1 (which is still orthogonal since the product of orthogonal matrices is orthogonal).

So we wish to show that 11 is an eigenvalue of either BB or ABAB.

Now ABAB is just BB but with the first row negated (and hence is also orthogonal). So by throwing in the −tI−tI for finding eigenvalues, we have

pAB(t)=(−a−t)P(t)–Q(t),pB(t)=(a−t)P(t)+Q(t),pAB(t)=(−a−t)P(t)–Q(t),pB(t)=(a−t)P(t)+Q(t),

for some (n−1)(n−1)th degree polynomials P,QP,Q. Note that BB and ABAB are invertible, so neither have an eigenvalue of 00. We also have pAB(0)=−pB(0)pAB(0)=−pB(0), neither of which are 00 since 00 is not an eigenvalue. So these have different sign. But the leading coefficients of pAB(t)pAB(t) and pB(t)pB(t) are the same, so one of these has at least two real roots, at least one of which is positive.

Note that for any orthogonal matrix CC, we have |Cv|=|v||Cv|=|v|, so all eigenvalues of CC have magnitude 11, and in particular if one of these eigenvalues if a positive real, then it is 11.

Hence one of ABAB and BB have an eigenvalue of 11, as required.
