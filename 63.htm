bijections

Bijections, as covered in the functional equations post (that is not a required reading in case you were wondering), are things that are injective and surjective. In essence, they are a one-to-one matching between two sets.

Yeah, real helpful. Enlightening stuff.

Let’s try that again.

Thus you can conclude that there are four fruits on the right.

How can you do that without “counting” the fruits though? You match each of them up with a digit from 1-4. Sure, this might seem like counting, and seem completely pointless, but let’s just go with it for now. It gets better later, promise.

A formal haha no hey you go away stop defacing my posts definition of bijection is that you find a one-to-one correspondence (so everything gets paired up) from each set to the other.

Let’s look at an example problem.

Prove that the number of ways of partitioning nn into mm parts is the same as the number of ways of partitioning nn into any number of parts, the largest of which has size mm.

How would you solve this question?

By finding an explicit formula for the the number of ways of splitting nn in each of the two ways, and being like “duh they’re the same”!

Nah, that just doesn’t work. I mean obviously it doesn’t because we’re in a bijections thing, but even if we weren’t, it still would’t work.

I mean okay yes it does work if you know what generating functions are or know how to write recursive formulas and prove that they’re the same but that’s beyond the scope of this course that’s like using the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra to prove that no other prime numbers divide 2, or if you want a more concrete example, using a nuke instead of a jackhammer to break concrete.

How do we solve this question? You should give it a shot first! But if you’ve really given up, scroll down —

Let’s start with some basics representation. We’ll represent a solution as follows: order the partitioned bits from biggest to smallest, and represent them as dots in columns. Here, have an example: 13=4+4+3+1+1

Now, rotate 90 degrees and see what happens!

If you still don’t get it, then think a bit more!

It’s pretty magic how a new perspective can really turn things around, right?

Bijections can be like that sometimes.

problems
  1. A guy wants to buy ten pieces of fruit from the store and there is an unlimited supply of each of apples, bananas, canteloupes, durianand and evergreen huckleberries. How many ways can they do this?
  2. A person has hung 13 coins as follows and wants to cut the strings so that the coins fall one at a time. In how many ways can they do so?
  3. How many rectangles are there on an 8×88×8 chessboard?
  4. In numberland, car plates have six-digit all-number (0-9) plates. If A is the number of cars where the sum of the first three digits is the same as the sum of the last three, and B is the number of cars where all the digits sum to 27, prove that A=B.
  5. Prove that the numbers of each of these are the same:
    The number of ways to get from the bottom left to top right of an n×nn×n chessboard, moving only right or up, without going below the diagonal.
    The number of triangulations of an n+1n+1-gon.
    The number of ways to arrange 2n2n distinct numbers in a 2×n2×n table so that each row and column is increasing.
  6. On a flat plane, taking only steps of north, south, east and west by one unit, how many ways are there for a wanderer to take 2n2n steps and end back where they started?
  7. A sequence of positive integers (not necessarily distinct) is called a good sequence if it satisfies the following condition: for each positive integer k≥2k≥2, if the number kk appears in the sequence then so does the number k−1k−1, and moreover the first occurrence of k−1k−1 comes before the last occurrence of kk. How many good sequences are there of length nn?
  8. Consider 2n2n vertices of a regular 2n2n-gon. Join nn pairs of points with directed straight lines each such that no two lines cross. A configuration is called good if for any four points, if AA points to BB and CC points to DD, then ABCDABCD do not appear in clockwise order. Count the number of good configurations.
  9. Prove that the number of ways to partition nn into odd positive integers and nn into distinct positive integers are the same.
  10. How many labelled trees are there of size nn?
    (a tree is a connected graph with no edges, labelled means that every vertex is labelled with a number from 1 to n, two graphs are the same if there exists a label-preserving isomorphism between them (i.e two edges in one graph are connected iff two are connected in the other graph) if you really don’t get what I’m talking about just look it up and then prove the formula).
