how to do olympiad geometry

Geometry in maths olympiads is fun for some, but for many others, it’s the cause of much pain and struggle in their olympiad career. If you’re one of those people, don’t worry – I was like that not too long ago. But don’t worry! I’ve got some tips on how to avoid some common pitfalls and improve your geometry skills. Soon, you too can easily solve insane geometry problems! cough2019 IMO Q6cough

Okay, that was a lie, I can’t guarantee you’ll suddenly be able to solve every IMO geometry problem ever, but whatever, here are some tips.

Things to avoid

The most common mistake for relatively inexperienced geometers is that they always spam their diagram with all the angles they can possibly find, and then think that the solution will always magically pop out. Unless the problem you’re doing is really easy, that almost never works. “But wait, what do I do if I don’t angle chase?” you may ask. Don’t worry, I’ll come to that a bit later.

Another thing you shouldn’t do is draw really complicated diagrams. If the question has a lot of points, it’s usually better to only draw parts of the diagram, and make deductions from there, maybe deleting a few points along the way once you extract useful information. After that, you can add the other points in.

Things to learn

Geometry is often about recognising configurations. You might learn many useful lemmas from books or from doing problems or however you do stuff, but being able to recognise important configurations in a diagram is really important. Make sure you learn all the important ones, and practice being able to spot and apply them.

You could also learn some cool techniques if you’re already decent at normal geometry. Stuff like inversion (inversion is op), poles/polars and projective geometry can be useful in many problems. Just make sure you know how to do the normal stuff well first.

Geoguessr

This is the really important one. Geometry is inherently about guessing things. It sounds sorta silly when I put it that way, but ultimately, harder geometry problems come down to making conjectures/observations about the diagram, and then proving them, and using these conjectures to prove other conjectures, etc, until you eventually finish the problem. It’s similar to any other topic, I guess.

Proving many of these individual conjectures is usually not too hard and can be done with some angle chasing or useful observations; the hard part is actually guessing the right things, and then chaining together a bunch of conjectures to solve a problem. This part is very intuitive, and you’ll get better at it as you do more problems. I often guess based on things that would be the coolest if true, which means often I guess things with terribly badly drawn diagrams because I guess more on gut feeling than on what looks true on the diagram. I am not recommending drawing bad diagrams in any way though – a lot of conjectures also come from noticing that three points on your diagram look collinear, or something that requires an accurate diagram to spot.

Practice

You’ve probably heard this from literally anyone ever, but the only way to improve is to practice. We both know it’s true, so I won’t say much more on this.

Bashing

Okay, so bashing can be useful. If you’re good at bashing, then more power to you. I personally never learned how to bash so I can’t really say anything definitive about it, but basically don’t pour all your energy into getting good at bashing. Bashes usually get 0 or 7, and they take really long. A lot of problems can’t be bashed either. Basically, bashing is another tool in your toolbox; it can be useful, but make sure you can do geometry synthetically, and only bash when appropriate. Although, I can confirm that you don’t need to be able to bash to be able to solve hard problems.
