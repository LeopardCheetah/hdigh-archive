Let’s do some real sequence stuff. I mean sequences of reals, not real sequences, although this post will just hit you with the real hard stuff.

Firstly, learning how to solve homogenous recurrence equations is helpful.

Suppose xixi is a sequence satisfying, for all k∈Nk∈N,

∑i=0daixk+i=0∑i=0daixk+i=0
for some fixed coefficients aiai. Then we say that
∑i=0daixi∑i=0daixi
is the characteristic polynomial for xixi.

Let us denote all roots of this polynomial (work in an algebraically field, using field extensions as needed) α1,α2,…,αtα1,α2,…,αt where root αiαi appears with multiplicity mimi. Then the general form of the solution to the recurrence is

xn=∑i=1t(∑j=0m−1ci,jnjαn)xn=∑i=1t(∑j=0m−1ci,jnjαn)
for some constants ca,bca,b, determined by x1,x2,…,xd,xd+1x1,x2,…,xd,xd+1

For a proof of this result, note that the set of all solutions to the initial recurrence form a vector space, and that all terms from xd+2xd+2 onwards are determined. Thus given d+1d+1 linearly independent sets of vectors, they must form a basis, and in fact the set of solutions formed when you let all but one of the ci,jci,js equal 00 is such a basis.

Some other things which are worth knowing are the definition of convergent sequences, as well as boundedness, and some results thereof.

Definition a subsequence of a sequence aiai is formed by taking a set i1<i2<i3<…i1<i2<i3<… of positive integers and letting bk=aikbk=aik.

In other words, a subsequence is any sequence you can get by deleting whatever terms you want from aiai.

Definition a sequence aiai is monotone if an+1−anan+1−an is always non-negative or always non-positive; it is strictly monotonic if it is always negative or always positive.

Definition a sequence aiai is bounded if there exists a real constant MM such that |ai|<M|ai|<M for all ii.

It is bounded above if there exists a real constant MM such that ai<Mai<M for all ii,
and bounded below if there exists a real constant MM such that M<aiM<ai for all ii.

Useful properties: periodic sequences are bounded, term-wise sums and products of bounded sequences are bounded, a sequence is bounded iff it is bounded above and below

Definition a sequence xixi is convergent if there exists x∈Rx∈R for all ϵ>0ϵ>0, there exists N∈NN∈N such that for all n>Nn>N,

|x−xn|<ϵ|x−xn|<ϵ

More useful stuff If xx exists, it is unique, and is called the limit of the sequence, and we write xi→xxi→x. If a series isn’t convergent then it is divergent. A series diverges to infinity if, for all reals MM, there exists some real NN such that for all n>Nn>N, xn>Mxn>M. (and to negative infinity if you write xn<Mxn<M instead).

Useful properties: term-wise sums and products and quotients and differences of limits of sequences almost always behave how you’d expect (exception being division on 0,00,0 and ∞,∞∞,∞ sequences and the like; sometimes they are defined), subsequences of a convergent series converge to the same limit

monotone bounded sequence theorem aka “mbst”, pronounced “mobster”

Any monotone bounded sequence is convergent.

Bolzano-Weierstrass Theorem

Every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence.

Squeeze Theorem

If an≤bn≤cnan≤bn≤cn for sufficiently large nn, and an,cn→Lan,cn→L, then bn→Lbn→L.

There’s also Cauchy’s criterion, ratio test, integral test, alternating series, etc. etc. for proving convergence of a series. Not really recommended reading material. Also limit inferiors and limit superiors are cool, but not required.

Basically just consider sequences as countably-infinite-dimensioned vectors in RNRN over the field RR and define everything naturally and limits and stuff behave as you’d expect.
